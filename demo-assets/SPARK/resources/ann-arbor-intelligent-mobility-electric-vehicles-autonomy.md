# Ann Arbor’s Advantage in Intelligent Mobility

## Introduction
Ann Arbor has built a strong foundation for intelligent mobility—where electric vehicles (EVs), autonomy, and connected systems can move from research into real-world deployment. For founders and growing companies, this matters because mobility innovation is rarely built in isolation: it depends on safe testing environments, specialized engineering talent, public-private coordination, and access to patient capital.

This guide is part of Ann Arbor SPARK’s resource suite supporting regional economic development and business growth—designed to help startups and scaling companies find practical startup resources, pilot pathways, and long-term business support in Ann Arbor.

## Why Ann Arbor is a Strategic Hub for Intelligent Mobility
Ann Arbor’s advantage comes from how the region connects deep research to real deployment—and then to commercialization.

Key signals of momentum include:

- **Autonomous trucking R&D and high-wage job creation:** TORC Robotics (a Daimler Truck subsidiary) opened a $5.6M facility in Ann Arbor Township supported by a $3M Michigan Strategic Fund grant, with plans to create up to 500 high-paying roles in engineering and machine learning (average salary reported at $177K). This is a strong indicator of the region’s ability to attract and retain advanced mobility teams. [Self-driving truck company opens $5.6 million Ann Arbor Twp. facility, creating 500 local jobs](https://concentratemedia.com/self-driving-truck-company-opens-56-million-ann-arbor-twp-facility-creating-500-local-jobs/)
- **Driverless operations in a complex city environment:** May Mobility expanded driverless operations to Ann Arbor, operating in and around approximately two square miles of surface streets—an important milestone for proving real-world performance. [May Mobility expands autonomous driver-out vehicle operations to second US city](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/may-mobility-expands-autonomous-driver-out-vehicle-operations-to-second-us-city-302310860.html)
- **A long-running connected vehicle testbed:** University of Michigan research helped position Ann Arbor as a test environment for connected and driverless systems, building local comfort with pilots, data collection, and shared fleet concepts. [Ann Arbor - a test bed for driverless cars?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eHfMfHyiWYA)

This demonstrates that for founders, Ann Arbor is a strategic location to validate, deploy, and hire.

## Physical Infrastructure & Testing Facilities
Mobility companies succeed faster when they can test safely, iterate quickly, and validate performance before expanding. Two major regional assets often factor into Ann Arbor-area commercialization plans:

- **University of Michigan test environments (including Mcity):** University-led mobility research and testing have helped normalize real-world pilots in the region and support a pipeline of engineers trained in connected and automated vehicle concepts. [Ann Arbor - a test bed for driverless cars?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eHfMfHyiWYA)
- **American Center for Mobility (ACM):** Located nearby, ACM is widely used for connected and automated vehicle testing and validation. For companies, the practical value is access to controlled environments that can strengthen safety cases, accelerate product readiness, and support customer confidence.

If your roadmap includes autonomy, V2X, ADAS, or EV platform controls, build testing and validation into your commercialization plan early—and align your milestones to the facilities that fit your operating design domain.

## Talent Pipeline: Hiring for EV, Autonomy, and Connected Systems
Ann Arbor’s mobility ecosystem is strengthened by a steady flow of engineering and technical talent.

### University of Michigan: research-to-real-world talent
The University of Michigan’s long-standing role in connected and driverless systems helps produce graduates and research talent aligned to autonomy, perception, safety systems, embedded controls, and mobility data work. That legacy also supports today’s pilots by normalizing real-world testing and applied validation. [Ann Arbor - a test bed for driverless cars?](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eHfMfHyiWYA)

Practical ways companies engage:
- Recruiting for internships and early-career roles in software, robotics, AI/ML, and embedded systems
- Collaborating with labs and research groups to strengthen validation, benchmarking, and safety methodologies

### Regional institutions that support scaling teams
Companies scaling operations often need more than PhDs and senior engineers. The broader region—including Eastern Michigan University and Washtenaw Community College—can be valuable for building teams in manufacturing support, electrical and mechanical technician roles, and operations.

Practical ways to build a pipeline:
- Establish internship and apprenticeship pathways
- Create clear role ladders (technician → lead → supervisor) to retain talent as you grow
- Hire locally for test operations, fleet support, and field service—roles that help move pilots into repeatable deployments

## Commercialization Playbook: Mobility-Specific Guidance (Built for Ann Arbor)
General startup advice is not enough for EV and autonomy companies. Investors and partners typically expect deeper operational readiness—especially around safety, validation, and deployments.

### 1) Build a “pilot-to-scale” plan that stands up to scrutiny
Before you raise or expand a pilot, prepare a package that includes:
- Operating design domain definition (where the system can and cannot operate)
- Pilot scope, timeline, and success metrics
- Stakeholder map (city/campus, operator, insurer, maintenance)
- Data plan (what you collect, how you store it, how you report outcomes)
- Safety and incident response plan
- Budget and resourcing (including who owns day-to-day operations)

### 2) Use local proof points—without copying their strategy
The strongest Ann Arbor stories are not just about technology; they’re about execution.

- TORC’s facility and hiring plans show how major mobility employers structure engineering growth around long-term productization. [Self-driving truck company opens $5.6 million Ann Arbor Twp. facility, creating 500 local jobs](https://concentratemedia.com/self-driving-truck-company-opens-56-million-ann-arbor-twp-facility-creating-500-local-jobs/)
- New Eagle / Pi Innovo illustrates a different commercialization path: embedded controls and platform-enabling engineering that supports electrification programs. [New Eagle - MiddleGround Capital](https://middleground.com/companies/new-eagle/)
- Bedestrian, Roadsr, and Arbor Batteries show that the region’s opportunity set includes adjacent categories—delivery systems, safety, and batteries—where commercialization may follow different timelines than autonomy. [Top Mobility Companies to Know in Michigan](https://www.purpose.jobs/blog/top-mobility-companies-michigan)

### 3) Match capital to mobility realities
Mobility capital stacks are often blended:
- **Early stage (pre-seed/seed):** Technical validation, a narrow deployment use case, and pilot partner intent.
- **Growth stage:** Repeatable deployments, reliability metrics, safety case maturity, and operational readiness.
- **Non-dilutive and incentives:** Consider Michigan tools where your hiring, deployment, and R&D plan align with state priorities. TORC’s Michigan Strategic Fund support is one example of how incentives can factor into major mobility investments. ## Practical Next Steps (30–60 Days)
Use this checklist to convert the Ann Arbor mobility ecosystem into measurable momentum:

- **Define your first repeatable deployment:** freight yard autonomy, last-mile delivery, microtransit, safety analytics, battery performance, embedded controls, or fleet data tooling.
- **Shortlist three pilot stakeholders:** (1) operator, (2) site owner (campus/municipality/private), (3) technical validation collaborator.
