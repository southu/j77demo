# Resources for Minority-, Immigrant-, and Women-Owned Businesses in Ann Arbor

## Introduction
Ann Arbor is widely recognized for research, entrepreneurship, and new company formation—and the region’s next chapter of business growth will *often* be shaped by whether founders of every background can access capital, coaching, and strong networks.

This guide curates practical **startup resources** and **business support** for minority-, immigrant-, and women-owned businesses across the Ann Arbor region, with an emphasis on founders building in high-growth sectors like AI, climate tech, biotech, and intelligent mobility.

If you’re building locally, you don’t need to navigate the ecosystem alone. Start with the resources below, then connect your business to the right funding, partners, and talent.

## Direct business coaching and small business support (Ann Arbor)
A strong support system can help you move faster—from validating your idea and setting up operations to meeting funders and landing customers.

### Ann Arbor SPARK: small business and startup support
Ann Arbor SPARK provides no-cost support services designed to help companies start, scale, and stay in the region. These services may include coaching and mentorship, connections across the ecosystem, and pathways to funding. Explore SPARK’s small business hub: [Small Business Resources - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/).

**What you can do next (practical steps):**
- Request coaching to sharpen your business model and go-to-market plan
- Ask for introductions to local service providers and partner organizations
- Explore potential fit with SPARK Capital (availability and eligibility can vary)

## Funding and grant opportunities (including grants for women-owned business Ann Arbor)
Funding can look different depending on your stage—from debt financing to non-dilutive grants.

### Michigan Women Forward: financing for women and entrepreneurs of color
**Michigan Women Forward** is a community development financial institution (CDFI) focused on expanding economic opportunity for women and entrepreneurs of color. Learn more through SPARK’s resource page: [Michigan Women Forward - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/michigan-women-forward/). You can also visit the organization directly: [Michigan Women Forward: Home](https://miwf.org).

**How to use this resource well:**
- Prepare a clear use-of-funds plan (what you’ll spend on in the next 6–12 months)
- Bring basic financials (even early-stage projections are helpful)
- Ask about coaching or technical assistance in addition to financing

### Non-dilutive funding: grants and programs to watch
Non-dilutive funding can be a strong fit for early validation, pilot projects, and operational upgrades.

- **Inclusive Entrepreneurship Support Grants (IESG):** Michigan’s IESG program awarded $8.8 million to support diverse small businesses and entrepreneurs, aiming to reduce barriers and help businesses create jobs and grow the economy. Track announcements and eligibility details here: [Gov. Whitmer Announces $8.8 Million to Support Diverse Small Businesses](https://www.michiganbusiness.org/press-releases/2024/05/inclusive-entrepreneurship/).
- **Amber Grants for Women:** WomensNet’s Amber Grants award three $10,000 grants monthly, with year-end grants up to $50,000. Details and application guidance: [Small Business Grants for Women in Michigan | Womensnet](https://ambergrantsforwomen.com/business-grants-women-michigan/).

**Application-ready checklist (grant-focused):**
- A one-paragraph problem and solution statement written in plain language
- A simple budget tied to outcomes (e.g., pilot launched, prototype built, hires made)
- Proof of traction (letters of intent, early sales, partnerships, pilots)
- A founder story that clearly answers: *Why you, why now, and why this business is positioned to win* (this is especially useful for narrative-based applications like Amber Grants)

## Government and regional programs (Washtenaw, Livingston, Monroe)
Public-facing programs can be helpful for hands-on navigation—especially when you’re balancing operations with fundraising and customer development.

### Regional Small Business Support program: coaching and navigation
A regional Small Business Support program serving Washtenaw, Livingston, and Monroe counties reported supporting 237 small businesses in its first year (more than 60% women- or minority-owned) and generating $185,000 in follow-on funding and grants while supporting new business launches. Review the program overview, outcomes, and participating partners here: [New program offers free support for entrepreneurs in Washtenaw, Livingston, Monroe Counties](https://concentratemedia.com/new-program-offers-free-support-for-entrepreneurs-in-washtenaw-livingston-monroe-counties/).

### City of Ann Arbor: economic development information and updates
The City of Ann Arbor’s economic development page is a reliable place to monitor small-business initiatives, news, and related updates as they are posted: [Economic Development | City of Ann Arbor](https://www.a2gov.org/economic-development/).

**Practical next step:**
- If your business is Ann Arbor-based, bookmark the City page and check periodically for small-business initiatives, procurement updates, and new programming.

## Immigrant entrepreneur mentorship Michigan (and culturally responsive support)
If you’re an immigrant entrepreneur, programs with community-connected mentoring and culturally responsive navigation can make day-to-day execution easier.

- APACC’s curated list includes immigrant and minority entrepreneurship programs and business mentoring resources, including ACCESS and other supports: [Entrepreneurial Resources](https://www.apacc.net/entrepreneurial-resources).

## Business certification guidance (MBE, WBE) and supplier readiness
Selling to larger customers—corporations, universities, and government agencies—often requires supplier readiness, certification, and the right introductions.

### Certifications and supplier readiness support
- **CEED (via the Ann Arbor/Ypsilanti Regional Chamber):** CEED is listed as a Chamber partner and is commonly associated with support for small businesses, including women- and minority-owned firms. For the most up-to-date information on CEED’s offerings and how to engage, start here: [Chamber Partners - Ann Arbor](https://www.a2ychamber.org/about-us/chamber-partners/).

**What to ask for (to prepare for MBE/WBE-style certification and larger buyers):**
- Guidance on which certifications you should pursue based on your target customers
- Help building a one-page capability statement (what you do, who you serve, proof points)
- Readiness support for common procurement requirements (insurance, invoicing, compliance basics)

## Networking and community organizations (relationships that lead to customers)
A strong local network is one of the fastest ways to find referrals, early customers, and trusted service providers.

- **Ann Arbor SPARK network connections:** SPARK can help you identify relevant founder communities, events, and partner organizations based on your stage and sector: [Small Business Resources - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/).
- **APACC’s resource list:** In addition to mentoring and programs, APACC’s list is a helpful starting point for finding community-connected entrepreneurship supports: [Entrepreneurial Resources](https://www.apacc.net/entrepreneurial-resources).

## Talent and workforce resources (local, actionable)
As you scale, hiring and retention become operational priorities—not side tasks. These resources can help you stabilize your workforce plan and reduce friction for you and your team.

- **SPARK connections for growth hiring:** SPARK can help you navigate local introductions and workforce-related partners as your team grows: [Small Business Resources - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/).
- **Employment and immigration-related support:** The Women’s Center of Southeastern Michigan highlights local resources, including SEMCA Michigan Works and the Michigan Immigrant Rights Center, which can be helpful when team members are navigating work-related barriers: [Immigration - The Women's Center of Southeastern Michigan](https://www.womenscentersemi.org/immigration).

## Local success story (Ann Arbor region)
Founders often tell us the most valuable part of the ecosystem isn’t a single program—it’s the sequence: a coaching conversation that sharpens the plan, followed by the right lender or partner introduction.

One common pathway in the Ann Arbor region is starting with **Ann Arbor SPARK’s** no-cost coaching and referrals, then exploring mission-aligned financing through **Michigan Women Forward** when the business has a clear use-of-funds plan and early traction ([Small Business Resources - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/), [Michigan Women Forward - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/michigan-women-forward/)).

## Understanding key ecosystem terms (plain-language guide)
These names come up often in founder conversations. Here is what they generally refer to:

- **SPARK Capital:** Ann Arbor SPARK’s capital-related efforts and connections that may help eligible companies explore funding pathways (availability and eligibility can vary).
- **University of Michigan:** A major local research university that can be a source of talent, research collaboration, and customer partnerships for some companies.
- **Michigan Innovate Capital Fund:** A State of Michigan initiative intended to expand access to capital for Michigan businesses.
- **Michigan Office of Future Mobility and Electrification:** A State of Michigan office focused on advancing mobility and electrification initiatives, which can matter for founders building in mobility-related sectors.

## Call to action
**Explore these resources, then connect with local leaders to move your business forward in Ann Arbor.** Start with SPARK’s hub: [Small Business Resources - Ann Arbor SPARK](https://annarborusa.org/small-business-resources/).
